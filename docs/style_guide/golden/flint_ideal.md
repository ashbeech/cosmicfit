# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Flint

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress the way a good editor writes. Nothing extra, nothing vague, every element there because it does a job. You notice a puckered seam or a sleeve cut half an inch too long before you notice the colour, and that exacting eye is your greatest asset.

Your wardrobe runs on a strict working principle. The core formula for your style is precise fit + honest fabric + one meticulous detail. Picture laundered cotton twill trousers with a perfect break, a washed oxford shirt in chalk white, and a single stainless steel watch with a face you can actually read. That is your baseline.

You build your wardrobe by refinement, not accumulation. Each season you replace the weakest piece with a better version of itself. Over time the collection sharpens like a well-kept knife.

Rely on the seam test as your ultimate compass. Turn any garment inside out before you buy it. If the seams are clean, finished, and straight, the maker was honest. If the inside is a mess, the outside is a lie, and you should leave it on the rack.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour logic is neutral in temperature and quiet in volume. You sit exactly at the point where warm and cool meet, which means clean neutrals serve you better than any committed temperature ever will.

Build your foundation with working neutrals that each carry a role. Graphite and soft stone anchor your trousers and outerwear. Oatmeal and greige act as your primary base canvas. Chalk white replaces brilliant white in your shirting. Dry sage bridges your layers and stops the neutrals going flat.

Your accent rule is disciplined and single-minded. Introduce one deep, clean tone per outfit, bottle green or dark chocolate, and let it do quiet work in a jumper, a bag, or a knitted tie. One is enough. Two is clutter.

When an outfit reads too dry, rely on pale butter. This is your relief colour. A pale butter knit under a greige jacket softens the precision without blurring it.

Pass over neon brights, glitter finishes, and fussy sugary pastels. They read as noise against your clean signal and undermine the exactness you work for.

Your biggest trap is the beige-out. When every piece sits at the same pale value, the outfit looks unfinished rather than minimal. Rescue it with a graphite anchor piece or a bottle green accent, and let one texture change, a ribbed knit against smooth twill, restore the depth.

---

## 3. The Textures

You judge fabric by its honesty. A material must be exactly what it claims to be, wear well, and ask little of you in return.

### The Good

Your best materials rank by durability, hand feel, and low maintenance:

**Laundered cotton twill**: The backbone of your trousers and chinos, crisp but never stiff, better with every wash.

**Washed oxford cloth**: Essential for shirting that looks correct at a desk and honest at a weekend table.

**Fine merino**: Your base layer and jumper staple, temperature-steady and resilient through years of wear.

**Dry-handle linen**: Excellent for summer shirts and trousers, its light wrinkle a sign of truth, not neglect.

**Tightly woven gabardine**: Brilliant for jackets and overcoats that shed drizzle and hold a precise line.

**Vegetable-tanned leather**: The only choice for belts and bags, ageing into a record of use.

**Ribbed cotton**: Perfect for knitted layers that add texture without adding bulk.

### The Bad

Clammy synthetics, pilling acrylic, and over-stretched blends that bag at the knee within a month all fail your standard. If a fabric needs special handling to look acceptable, it has already cost you more than its price tag. You will resent it, then abandon it.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing sets a dry, crisp surface against a soft, washed one. Laundered twill trousers with a fine merino crew neck create quiet contrast that reads as care, not effort.

Use the recovery test before you buy. Crush a handful of the fabric for five seconds and release. If it recovers cleanly, it will serve you. If it stays crumpled, so will the garment by mid-morning.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of precise fit + honest fabric + one meticulous detail holds in every setting. You never need a separate costume, only a recalibration.

### At Work

Professional settings reward your exactness. Choose graphite trousers with a clean break, a chalk white oxford, and a fine merino layer in dry sage. The meticulous detail is your watch, or a belt whose buckle sits perfectly centred. People will trust your work before you speak, because your clothes already demonstrate your standards.

### Intimate Energy

For dinners and evenings, soften the precision without losing it. A washed linen shirt in oatmeal, open at the collar, over clean dark trousers. The detail becomes more personal here, a fine chain worn inside the collar or a beautifully made ring. Ease, executed exactly.

### Daily Movement

Errands and weekends get the same respect. A hardware store run deserves the same clean fit as a Monday meeting. Choose ribbed cotton knits, twill trousers or honest denim with a straight leg, and leather trainers kept genuinely clean.

The formula never bends: precise fit + honest fabric + one meticulous detail, at every hour of your week.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware in your wardrobe is functional first, decorative never. Every metal piece should look like a well-made tool.

### The Metals

Your zone is cool and matt. Brushed steel, matt nickel, and gunmetal suit your palette and your temperament. These finishes look engineered rather than styled, and they wear their scratches with dignity.

Skip ornate gold, decorative filigree, and anything mirror-polished. High shine draws the eye away from fit and fabric, which is exactly backwards for your style.

### The Stones

You barely need stones at all, and that restraint is correct. When you do choose one, make it small, dark, and flawless, matt black onyx or a deep grey spinel in a plain bezel setting. One stone, perfectly set, says more about your standards than a cluster ever could.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test. A buckle, watch, or clasp that feels hollow in your hand will fail you at the worst moment. Quality hardware has the calm, dense weight of a good tool. Looks lie. Weight doesn't.

## 6. The Code

A working checklist to keep your wardrobe exact and honest.

### Lean Into

Building every outfit on precise fit + honest fabric + one meticulous detail.

Turning garments inside out before buying. The seam test never lies.

Replacing the weakest piece in your wardrobe with a better version of itself, one at a time.

Matt, brushed, tool-like hardware that works as well as it looks.

### Avoid

Trend pieces that need explaining. If it requires context, it fails.

High-maintenance fabrics. Anything that demands hand-washing, constant steaming, or special hangers will end up unworn.

Visual clutter, extra straps, decorative zips, and logos doing nothing.

### Consider

The maintenance test. Before buying, ask exactly what this piece needs from you every week. If the answer annoys you in the shop, it will infuriate you at home.

Focusing on cost-per-wear. A gabardine coat worn two hundred times beats three cheap coats that each failed within a winter.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. Ask whether you would still wear this piece in five to ten years. If you hesitate, the answer is no.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories are where your formula finishes: the one meticulous detail that completes your precise fit and honest fabric. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look, each with a genuine job.

A proper watch is your signature category. Choose a clean face, a brushed steel case, and a leather or steel strap with no decorative complications. It is the single most Flint object you can own: precise, honest, and permanently useful.

Belts must be exact. Vegetable-tanned leather in a medium width, with a matt brushed buckle, cut to your actual size so the tongue lands on the middle hole. A belt on the wrong hole is a fit error you would notice on anyone else.

Bags should be structured and utilitarian. A canvas and leather tote or a clean briefcase in dark tan or graphite, with solid stitching and hardware that operates smoothly. No dangling charms, no branding.

Use the function test before leaving the house. Point at each accessory and name its job. If you cannot, take it off. Decoration without duty is clutter.

---

## 8. The Pattern

Your approach to pattern is measured and legible. Pattern must behave like good typography: present, precise, and never the headline.

For tailored pieces like jackets and trousers, keep pattern in the weave. Fine needle stripes, micro checks, and subtle herringbone in graphite or stone add quiet depth that rewards a second look.

For fluid pieces like shirts and knits, restrict yourself to tonal micro-dots or narrow, evenly spaced stripes in your neutral palette. The repeat should be regular enough to satisfy your eye for order.

Pass over splashy prints, novelty motifs, and oversized florals entirely. They are noise, and your entire style is built on signal.

Rely on the desk test. Look at the pattern from across a desk, about arm's length and a half. If it reads as clean texture, it passes. If individual motifs jump out and wave, it fails.
