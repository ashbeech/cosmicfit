# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Moss

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress for permanence. A garment interests you in proportion to how well it will survive a decade of actual life: weather, work, repair, and return. You would rather own one coat with a biography than five with price tags.

Your wardrobe is built like a drystone wall, piece by piece and made to stay. The core formula for your style is enduring structure + sensory comfort + one living texture. Picture a waxed cotton field jacket over a heavy cream hand-knit, walnut brown corduroy trousers, and boots whose leather has taken years of your walking. That is your baseline.

You build your collection deliberately and almost reluctantly. A new piece must argue its way in past everything you already own, and the argument that wins is always durability plus touch.

Rely on the decade test as your ultimate compass. Hold the garment and ask one question: will this look better in ten years than it does today? Wax, leather, wool, and good corduroy answer yes. Most things answer no, and your wardrobe is built from the things that answer yes.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour world is warm, deep, and grown rather than dyed. You thrive in the tones of woodland and field, colours that improve with fading the way your fabrics improve with wear.

Build your foundation with living earth tones in working roles. Walnut brown and dark olive anchor your outerwear and trousers. Oatmeal cream acts as your primary base canvas. Bark brown and warm stone bridge your layers, and deep moss green ties the whole territory together.

Your accent rule is seasonal and singular. Introduce one dried, warm tone per outfit, russet, dried aubergine, or old gold. A russet scarf against dark olive is the exact note of autumn your palette wants, and one note is plenty.

When a look turns too dense, rely on washed denim blue. This is your relief colour. A soft blue shirt under heavy earth tones opens the outfit the way sky opens a landscape.

Pass over neon brights, icy pastels, and hard jet black worn head to toe. They read as synthetic against a palette that is otherwise entirely organic.

Your biggest trap is the all-earth sink. Brown on olive on bark, all matt and all heavy, can swallow you whole. When the outfit starts reading as undergrowth, lift it: an oatmeal cream layer at the collar, one piece of polished leather, or the washed blue relief to let in some air.

---

## 3. The Textures

Texture is your true palette. You evaluate materials by hand, by weight, and by how they will record time.

### The Good

Your best materials rank strictly by endurance and touch:

**Waxed cotton**: Your signature outer layer, weatherproof, repairable, and more personal with every scar.

**Heavyweight wool**: Essential for coats and overshirts that shelter you for decades, not seasons.

**Corduroy**: The king of your trouser drawer, warm, tactile, and unmistakably alive in low light.

**Undyed linen**: Your summer counterweight, honest, breathable, and better creased than pressed.

**Chunky hand-knit wool**: The living texture your formula calls for, one jumper doing the work of an entire outfit.

**Nubuck and oiled leather**: For boots and bags that mould to your life and keep the diary for you.

**Felted wool**: Perfect for hats and dense outer layers that hold warmth and shape in real weather.

### The Bad

Slippery synthetics, paper-thin fast-fashion knits, and plasticky coated fabrics fail every test you own. They record nothing, repair as nothing, and age into landfill instead of character. If it cannot be mended, it should not be bought.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing sets a rugged outer against a soft inner. A waxed jacket over a fine merino or brushed cotton layer gives you weatherproof structure with comfort at the skin, which is your entire philosophy in two layers.

Use the patina test before you buy. Ask how this material will wear its damage. If scuffs will read as history, buy it. If they will read as ruin, leave it.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of enduring structure + sensory comfort + one living texture works everywhere, because permanence is never out of place.

### At Work

Professional settings get your structure at its cleanest. Dark olive wool trousers, an oatmeal knit over a soft-collared shirt, and a heavyweight blazer in bark brown. The living texture arrives as a corduroy or felted wool detail. You look like the person who will still be reliable in ten years, because you are.

### Intimate Energy

Evenings soften the structure and raise the touch. A chunky cream hand-knit with dark tailored trousers, or a brushed wool overshirt in deep moss worn open. Fabrics that invite a hand resting on your arm do more here than any shine ever could.

### Daily Movement

Weekends are your home ground. Walking the dog deserves the same standard as a meeting. Waxed cotton, corduroy, hand-knits, and boots that have earned their creases. Your errand clothes and your best clothes are close relatives, which is precisely the point.

The formula of enduring structure + sensory comfort + one living texture never changes. Only the weather does.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware in your wardrobe should look dug up rather than bought, warm metal with weather in it.

### The Metals

Your zone is warm and weathered. Aged brass, antiqued copper, and dark bronze suit your palette and your patience. These metals develop patina on exactly the same schedule as your leather and wax, so the whole wardrobe ages in step.

Skip bright chrome and shiny gold plate. A mirror finish on a waxed jacket is a wristwatch on a statue, technically possible and completely wrong.

### The Stones

Choose stones with earth in them. Tiger's eye, green agate, amber, and jasper carry warm depth without a single flash. Set them in aged brass or dark silver and wear them rarely; one ring with some geology in it outranks a drawer of sparkle.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test. A buckle, clasp, or zip that feels hollow will not survive your decade test, let alone your actual decades. Good hardware feels like a small tool. Looks lie. Weight doesn't.

---

## 6. The Code

A steady set of rules to keep your wardrobe permanent and alive.

### Lean Into

Building every outfit on enduring structure + sensory comfort + one living texture.

Buying once and repairing forever. Rewaxing, resoling, and darning are part of ownership.

Warm earth tones lifted by oatmeal cream and one dried-russet note.

Weathered brass and bronze that age alongside your leather.

### Avoid

Fast trend pieces with a two-season lifespan. Your wardrobe thinks in decades.

Synthetic fabrics that cannot take a repair or a memory.

The all-earth sink, heavy matt tones with no cream, no sky, and no lift.

### Consider

How each piece will record time. Ask what this garment will look like after fifty wears, and buy only if the answer is better.

Focusing on cost-per-wear. A waxed jacket rewaxed every winter for fifteen years is the cheapest coat you will ever own.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. For you the question is nearly rhetorical, but ask it anyway. If a piece cannot promise you ten years, it does not get one week.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories are where your formula lands its final term, the one living texture that completes your enduring structure and sensory comfort. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look, each built to outlast you.

Leather bags are your headline category. A full-grain satchel or field bag in walnut or dark tan, with aged brass fittings and stitching you could repair yourself. It should look slightly better every single year you own it.

Belts do structural work. Wide, vegetable-tanned leather with a solid weathered brass buckle. The belt is a load-bearing wall in your outfits, never a decoration.

Hats finish the silhouette. A felted wool wide-brim in bark brown or a waxed cotton field hat, chosen in plain shapes with no trim. A good hat in your world is workwear that happens to flatter.

Use the heirloom test before leaving the house. Look at each accessory and ask who you could leave it to. If the answer is nobody, because it will not last, it has no place in your wardrobe.

---

## 8. The Pattern

Your approach to pattern is heritage and landscape. A pattern earns its place by looking like it existed before you were born.

For tailored pieces like jackets, coats, and trousers, choose heritage checks, gun club weaves, and earth-toned herringbone. The pattern should live in the wool itself, visible at a handshake and invisible across a field.

For fluid pieces like shirts and scarves, choose tonal botanical prints, leaf motifs on dark grounds, and quiet plaids in moss and bark. Nothing that would look out of place folded on a farmhouse chair.

Pass over neon graphics, glossy animal prints, and any motif invented last season. Your patterns should read like landscape, not billboard.

Rely on the field test. Look at the garment from across the room and soften your eyes. If the pattern settles into a natural texture, like ground seen from a hill, it is yours. If it flashes for attention, it is not.
