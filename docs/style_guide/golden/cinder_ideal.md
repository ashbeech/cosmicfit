# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Cinder

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress for the hour after sunset. Your presence does not ask for daylight; it supplies its own heat from somewhere darker. You want clothes with intent, cut close to the bone, that make an entrance without saying a word.

Your wardrobe burns on a controlled fuel line. The core formula for your style is dark drama + sharp edges + one molten flash. Picture a razor-shouldered black leather jacket over a jet silk camisole, cigarette trousers with a knife crease, and a single heavy molten gold cuff catching the bar light. That is your baseline.

You build your wardrobe decisively, in strikes rather than seasons. When you see the right piece you know within seconds, and you are almost always correct. The mistakes happen when you deliberate, not when you commit.

Rely on the entrance test as your ultimate compass. Put the outfit on and walk through a door, any door. If you do not feel the room tilt one degree toward you, the outfit is underpowered. Take it apart and raise the stakes.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour world is dark-warm and high stakes. You operate in embers, not sunshine: black with heat behind it, and one flame allowed through at a time.

Build your foundation with nocturnal tones in strict roles. Jet black and ink carry your tailoring and leather. Deep aubergine and oxblood anchor your knits and evening pieces. Charcoal acts as your primary softening dark, and smoke grey bridges the layers when black on black needs a half-step.

Your accent rule is pyrotechnic and singular. Introduce one molten flash per outfit: scarlet, flame orange, or liquid gold. A scarlet silk lining glimpsed in a black coat, or one gold collar necklace against jet, is a controlled burn. Two flames is a house fire; never light the second.

When a look runs too hot or too hard, rely on smoked taupe. This is your relief colour. One matt taupe layer under dark drama keeps the outfit human without extinguishing it.

Pass over washed-out pastels, beige mediocrity, and muddy mid-tones. Anything apologetic on the hanger will be twice as apologetic on you.

Your biggest trap is costume. Leather, studs, red lip, gold hardware, and a smoky eye all at once tips drama into fancy dress. When you feel the look becoming a character, hold the line at one molten flash and return every other element to matt black. Restraint is what separates menace from panto.

---

## 3. The Textures

You need materials with body and conviction. A fabric earns its place by holding a sharp line in low light and surviving a long night without wilting.

### The Good

Your best materials rank strictly by edge and endurance:

**Leather**: Your foundation and your armour, for jackets, trousers, and skirts that hold a silhouette like a drawn blade.

**Dense wool crepe**: Essential for tailoring with sharp shoulders and knife creases that last past midnight.

**Heavy silk satin**: The liquid counterpoint, for camisoles and linings that gleam darkly under low light.

**Cut velvet**: Brilliant for evening pieces, absorbing light in the pile and flashing it back along the seams.

**Coated denim**: Your off-duty edge, matt black with a faint sheen, sharper than any distressed wash.

**Silk with weight**: For shirts that fall in decisive folds instead of fluttering.

**Sharp gabardine**: Perfect for cigarette trousers that keep their crease through a full night standing.

### The Bad

Flimsy jersey, fluffy pastel knits, and crinkled boho gauze are surrender documents. They droop where you need edges and whisper where you need intent. If a fabric cannot hold a shape at 1 a.m., it cannot hold your look at all.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing sets matt black armour against one liquid gleam. A matt leather jacket over a heavy satin camisole is your formula rendered in texture: dark drama, sharp edge, molten flash.

Use the shadow test before you buy. Take the garment away from the bright rail lighting and look at it in shade. If the silhouette still reads strong and the surface still shows life, it is yours. If it dies in shadow, it will die on you.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of dark drama + sharp edges + one molten flash does not soften for daylight. It calibrates.

### At Work

Professional settings get the drama at its most disciplined. A sharply structured black or aubergine suit, a silk shell, and the molten flash reduced to one gold signet or a scarlet lining only you know about. You will read as formidable rather than theatrical, which is exactly the correct dosage.

### Intimate Energy

Evenings are your native territory. Cut velvet in oxblood, heavy satin on the bias, leather cut close, and the flash worn openly, a molten gold collar or a scarlet heel. Choose fabrics that reward proximity and low light, because that is where you will be.

### Daily Movement

Errands do not get a ceasefire. A midnight petrol station run still deserves a silhouette. Coated denim, a jet knit, sharp boots, and sunglasses with conviction. The flash can shrink to a gold zip pull, but it rides along.

The formula of dark drama + sharp edges + one molten flash is constant. Only the wattage moves.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware is your weapons cabinet. It splits cleanly into two registers, and the split is the whole trick.

### The Metals

Your structural metal is cool and severe: gunmetal, blackened steel, and dark silver for zips, buckles, eyelets, and bag fittings. It should look engineered, like the chassis of the outfit.

Your personal metal is the molten flash itself: high-polish yellow gold, worn as one heavy, deliberate piece, a cuff, a collar, a signet. Against the blackened base, one polished gold object detonates perfectly.

Skip dainty rose gold, antiqued trinkets, and anything described as delicate. Your hardware carries the drama; it cannot afford to whisper.

### The Stones

You need stones that burn dark. Ruby, deep garnet, black diamond, and onyx hold fire inside darkness, which is your entire palette in miniature. Cuts must be sharp and settings confident. Avoid pale, cloudy, or raw stones; nothing about you is unfinished.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test. A gold-look cuff that feels like a shell casing without the brass is a bluff, and bluffs get called under bar light. Your flash piece especially must be dense enough to mean it. Looks lie. Weight doesn't.

---

## 6. The Code

A strike list to keep your drama sharp and your fire controlled.

### Lean Into

Building every outfit on dark drama + sharp edges + one molten flash.

Matt black layered on black with one texture shift, leather against crepe, satin against wool.

Blackened structural hardware with a single polished gold detonation.

Tailoring with shoulders, creases, and conviction.

### Avoid

Washed-out colours and apologetic fits. Dilution is the only thing that can actually beat you.

The second flash. One gold piece, one scarlet note, never both.

Costume drift: studs plus leather plus red plus gold plus attitude equals fancy dress.

### Consider

The endurance of each piece. Ask whether the crease, the shoulder, and the shine will still be standing at the end of a long night. Your clothes must outlast the evening.

Focusing on cost-per-wear. One perfectly cut leather jacket worn for fifteen years is the cheapest drama you will ever buy.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. Ask whether the piece will still cut a silhouette in five to ten years. Edges date slower than embellishments; buy the edge.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories are where your formula strikes its final match, the one molten flash that completes your dark drama and sharp edges. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look, and only one of them may burn.

Statement jewellery is your headline category. A heavy polished gold cuff, a rigid collar, or a signet with a dark stone. Worn singly, against black, at maximum confidence. This is the flash; treat it like the only spotlight on the stage.

Footwear is your edge made visible. Pointed boots, a stark stiletto, or a heavy-soled black boot with blackened hardware. The shoe should look like a decision, never an afterthought.

Bags must be structured and armed. A rigid top-handle or a sharp shoulder bag in black leather with gunmetal fittings. Soft, slouchy bags surrender your silhouette at the last step; carry architecture instead.

Use the blackout test before leaving the house. Imagine the lights cut and one beam finds you. Whichever piece that beam should land on, keep. If two pieces are fighting for the beam, remove the weaker one.

---

## 8. The Pattern

You treat pattern as weaponry, rarely drawn and always sharpened. Most of your wardrobe is solid; when pattern appears, it appears armed.

For tailored pieces like suits and coats, choose bold pinstripe on black, tuxedo stripes, and deep tonal-on-tonal jacquards that only reveal themselves at close range, like a threat politely worded.

For fluid pieces like silk shirts and linings, use dark animal, black-on-black leopard or deep oxblood python print, and abstract flame-lick motifs kept inside your ember palette.

Pass over ditsy florals, pastel tie-dye, and whimsical novelty prints. Whimsy is a fine quality in other people.

Rely on the low-light rule. Look at the pattern in dim light from ten feet. If it still reads as intent, wear it. If it collapses into mush, it lacks the edge your wardrobe runs on.
