# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Tide

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress to feel sheltered and secure in your own space. Before a garment even registers visually, you evaluate how it envelops your body. You seek pieces that wrap, pool, and protect.

Your wardrobe is built on a specific, intuitive rhythm. The core formula for your style is close drape + hidden depth + one warm point of light. Picture a voluminous midnight espresso cashmere wrap sweater falling over a bias cut dark plum silk slip dress, anchored entirely by a single antique bronze pendant resting against your collarbone.

You build your collection slowly and privately. You require time to live in a fabric before committing to it. A functional wardrobe for you acts as a private closed system.

Rely on the shelter test for every new piece. Put the garment on and close your eyes. If you feel exposed, rigid, or constricted, take it off immediately. Trust the pieces that make you feel safely anchored within yourself.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour world is warm, deep, and inherently private. You thrive in tonal richness that absorbs light rather than reflecting it.

Build your foundation with heavy, atmospheric darks that serve specific structural roles. Bitter chocolate and blackened indigo anchor your coats and trousers. Dark mahogany acts as your primary base canvas. Deep forest green bridges your layers. These shades provide the protective layer you seek.

Your accent rule is deeply deliberate and tightly controlled. You need only one warm, glowing tone per outfit. A scarf in burnt amber or a bag in rich cognac provides all the necessary heat.

When a look feels too heavy, rely on a soft, misty taupe. This is your relief colour. Use it as a fine knit layer under a heavy coat to give the outfit breathing room without breaking your protective silhouette.

Pass over stark optic white, harsh neons, and icy pastels. These colours are too loud and will pierce your carefully constructed quietness.

Your biggest trap is drowning in formless, head to toe black. When you wear uninterrupted dark layers, your silhouette can quickly look heavy and withdrawn. Break it with a warm point of light, like a hammered gold cuff or a tortoiseshell comb, to pull the focus back and restore dimension.

---

## 3. The Textures

Fabric is your first filter. You evaluate materials by how heavily they pool and how softly they yield to your physical form.

### The Good

Your best materials rank strictly by their capacity to drape and shelter:

**Heavy silk crepe**: Essential for dresses and blouses that trace the body with fluid, hidden depth.

**Brushed alpaca**: The ultimate choice for enveloping winter coats and oversized cardigans.

**Fine wool crepe**: Brilliant for trousers and skirts that pool heavily without collapsing.

**Fluid jersey**: Your foundation for base layers that move like water against the skin.

**Cupro**: The perfect bridge fabric for soft shirting and fluid trousers that demand a liquid drape.

**Washed velvet**: Perfect for evening pieces, providing tonal richness that absorbs the light beautifully.

**Soft suede**: A tactile, matt finish for footwear and bags that feels grounded and intimate.

### The Bad

Stiff, architectural fabrics will immediately make you feel trapped. Rigid raw denim, stiff organza, heavy canvas, and unyielding synthetic plastics force your body into a shape rather than flowing with it. If a material holds its own shape when you take it off, it is entirely wrong for your fluid style.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing happens when a protective, heavy knit shelters a liquid, pooling fabric. A thick alpaca wrap coat worn over a heavy silk crepe skirt creates the exact enveloping tension you need.

Use the collapse test in the fitting room. Drop the garment onto a chair. If it puddles into a fluid, soft shape, it belongs in your wardrobe. If it stands up rigidly on its own, walk away.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of close drape + hidden depth + one warm point of light translates seamlessly into any environment.

### At Work

Professional settings require you to maintain your protective layer without losing your edge. Choose relaxed, fluid trousers in dark mahogany paired with a beautifully draped wrap blouse. Add a single hammered bronze cuff. The tailoring is soft, keeping your physical space private and secure.

### Intimate Energy

For evening settings, lean fully into hidden depth. A bias cut washed velvet dress in blackened plum creates a subtle, tonal richness. The fabrics should pool around your ankles and invite closer inspection.

### Daily Movement

Weekends and errands require the exact same philosophy of shelter. Even on a late night grocery run, you need clothes that hold you comfortably, because a trip for milk does not require abandoning your silhouette. Choose fluid jersey trousers and a soft, oversized wool wrap sweater.

The formula holds across all contexts; only the volume of the drape changes.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware acts as the solitary beacon in your dark, fluid outfits. It provides necessary grounding and visual warmth.

### The Metals

Your metal strategy requires a warm, muted dominant approach. Seek out aged brass, hammered bronze, antiqued copper, and patinated gold. These finishes look as though they have been submerged in water and aged over time. They bring a quiet, historical weight to your fluid silhouettes.

Completely exclude high shine chrome, icy silver, and polished platinum. They are too sharp and clinical for your atmospheric style.

### The Stones

Look for stones that mimic the depth of water. Labradorite, dark amber, smoky quartz, and deep garnets possess the hidden depth you require. Avoid brilliant, highly faceted diamonds or clear glass stones that flash aggressively. You want stones that glow softly from within.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test. If a brass clasp, a bronze pendant, or a bag chain feels hollow or light in your hand, it fails immediately. Your hardware must possess genuine physical density to anchor your enveloping fabrics.

## 6. The Code

A private set of rules to keep your wardrobe feeling secure and aligned.

### Lean Into

Building outfits around the core formula of close drape + hidden depth + one warm point of light.

Soft, unstructured silhouettes that move fluidly with your body.

Tonal richness achieved by layering dark, atmospheric colours like espresso and indigo.

Warm, patinated metals that provide a quiet, ancient glow.

### Avoid

Rigid, architectural tailoring that forces you into a box.

Stark, high contrast colour blocking that disrupts your fluid lines.

Loud, aggressively sparkling accessories that demand public attention.

### Consider

Evaluating the physical comfort of a piece by asking if the garment protects your personal space and feels like a safe harbour.

Prioritising cost per wear. A beautifully made heavy silk skirt is a true investment compared to a cheap replacement that quickly loses its shape.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. Ask yourself if you would still wear this piece in five to ten years before bringing it into your closet.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories represent the final phase of your formula, supplying the one warm point of light required to balance your close drape + hidden depth. You do not need clutter. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look.

Soft, unstructured bags are essential. Choose slouchy hobos or large leather pouches in buttery, supple leather or thick suede. The bag should tuck seamlessly under your arm, feeling like a natural extension of your body.

Generous wraps and shawls serve as your ultimate protective tool. A massive, fringed cashmere wrap or a deep toned pashmina provides immediate shelter and movement.

Substantial amulets and pendants are your primary jewellery category. A heavy, tactile stone set in aged brass worn on a long chain provides the perfect anchor against a dark, fluid top.

Use the anchor test before leaving the house. Touch your chosen accessory. If it feels grounding and deeply personal, you are ready. If it feels like a heavy costume piece, take it off.

---

## 8. The Pattern

Your approach to pattern is subtle, shifting, and deeply private. You want patterns that look like shadows, water stains, or natural patinas.

For tailored pieces like soft coats or relaxed trousers, rely on tonal jacquards and mottled weaves. The pattern should be woven into the fabric itself, creating a textured depth that is only visible up close.

For fluid pieces like blouses, dresses, and wraps, look for soft ombré fades, watercolour washes, and clouded prints. The edges of the pattern must bleed and blur seamlessly into the background.

Pass over sharp geometrics, high contrast stripes, and stiff, repetitive polka dots. These patterns are far too rigid and loud for your style.

Rely on the twilight test. Look at the pattern in dim lighting. If the pattern merges beautifully into a rich, deep texture, it works. If it still shouts loudly in the dark, it disrupts your visual quietness and should be left behind.
