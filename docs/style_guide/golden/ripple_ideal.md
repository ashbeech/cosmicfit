# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Ripple

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress by touch, but you finish by feel. A garment must first pass through your hands, the way all good fabric should, and then it must soften around you like warm water closing over a stone. Sharp is never your destination; settled is.

Your wardrobe follows one melting principle. The core formula for your style is soft structure + blurred edges + a touch of quiet depth. Picture an unlined camel wool blazer worn over a fluid fawn slip skirt, the lapels rolling instead of standing, finished with a single dark garnet ring. That is your baseline.

You build your collection slowly and by immersion. A new piece spends its first weeks being worn at home, softening, until it stops feeling new. Only then does it join the outside rotation.

Rely on the melt test as your ultimate compass. Put the tailored piece on and roll your shoulders forward slowly. If the garment moves with you and resettles gently, it belongs to you. If it holds its position like scaffolding while you move inside it, it was cut for someone else's guide.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour world is warm, misted, and gentle. This surprises people who expect watery charts to run cold; your warmth comes from your Venus, and it softens everything it touches like late sun through sea fog.

Build your foundation with warm, low-contrast tones in quiet roles. Dark walnut and soft bark anchor your outerwear and knits. Mushroom taupe acts as your primary base canvas. Oatmeal stands in for white everywhere. Warm dove grey and gentle fawn bridge the layers so nothing ever meets anything abruptly.

Your accent rule is submerged and singular. Introduce one deep, quiet tone per outfit, rosewood, dark plum, or a muted brick. A dark plum knit under a mushroom coat is the whole gesture; the depth does its work without raising its voice.

When a look turns too heavy or too brown, rely on soft shell pink. This is your relief colour. One pale warm layer at the collar or wrist opens the outfit like light reaching shallow water.

Pass over icy pastels, optic white, and hard black. They put edges back into a wardrobe you have carefully taken the edges out of.

Your biggest trap is the sofa drift. Soften every piece far enough and one day you are simply in loungewear, everywhere. When the outfit stops holding any line at all, restore exactly one: a rolled but real lapel, a trouser with a soft crease, or a firm leather bag against all that give.

---

## 3. The Textures

Fabric is where your two instincts meet: the hand of an earth Venus, the flow of a water chart. You want substance that yields.

### The Good

Your best materials rank by weight-with-softness:

**Unstructured wool flannel**: The heart of your tailoring, holding a shape the way memory does, gently and approximately.

**Brushed cashmere**: Essential for knits that blur your silhouette while keeping real warmth against you.

**Washed silk**: Your fluid layer, matt and heavy enough to pour rather than flutter.

**Soft suede**: For jackets and bags, all the substance of leather with none of its argument.

**Double-faced jersey**: Perfect for dresses and skirts that follow the body a half-second behind.

**Garment-dyed cotton drill**: Your trouser staple, structure that has already been through the wash and come out kinder.

**Fine boiled wool**: Brilliant for coats with soft round shoulders and no hard interfacing anywhere.

### The Bad

Crisp poplin, hard raw denim, and anything bonded or coated fight your entire premise. If a fabric holds a knife crease proudly, it will spend its life arguing with the rest of your wardrobe. Equally, avoid flimsy chiffon; blur is not the same as vanishing.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing puts softened structure over true fluidity. An unlined flannel blazer over a washed silk slip gives you a line and a current in the same outfit, which is your formula rendered in cloth.

Use the melt test at the rail exactly as in the fitting room. Crush the shoulder of the garment gently in one hand. It should give like something living and recover slowly. A crackle or a fight means no.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of soft structure + blurred edges + a touch of quiet depth moves through your week without ever hardening.

### At Work

Professional settings get your structure at its most legible. The unlined blazer in dark walnut, a fine knit instead of a shirt, drill trousers with a soft crease. The quiet depth sits at your hand, one dark garnet ring. You read as calm and considered, which is exactly what you are.

### Intimate Energy

Evenings let the edges blur further. Washed silk in dark plum, brushed cashmere sliding off one shoulder, suede against candlelight. Fabrics that reward touch matter more here than anything the eye can verify from across the room.

### Daily Movement

Weekends stay soft and substantial. A rainy Sunday paper run deserves the same gentle standard. Double-faced jersey, a boiled wool coat, and suede boots that have long since moulded to you.

Whatever the setting, the formula of soft structure + blurred edges + a touch of quiet depth holds. Only the ratio of line to blur shifts.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware in your wardrobe is warm, small, and half-asleep. It should glow through the layers, never glint over them.

### The Metals

Your zone is warm and muted. Brushed gold, warm bronze, and satin-finish rose gold settle into your palette like embers into ash. Choose small-scale pieces with soft, rounded forms; nothing angular, nothing mirror-bright.

Skip polished chrome, bright silver, and any hard high-shine finish. A sharp flash of cool metal cuts through your careful blur like a torch beam.

### The Stones

Look for stones with depth and a low voice. Dark garnet, smoky quartz, warm pearl, and amber hold their colour inside themselves, which is where your accent rule lives. The garnet is your signature, the touch of quiet depth made wearable. Avoid brilliant white sparkle; it argues.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test. A soft aesthetic is not an excuse for soft quality: a rounded bronze ring should still land in your palm with real density. If it feels like a button, it will wear like one. Looks lie. Weight doesn't.

---

## 6. The Code

A gentle set of rules to keep your wardrobe soft without letting it dissolve.

### Lean Into

Building every outfit on soft structure + blurred edges + a touch of quiet depth.

Warm misted neutrals, walnut through mushroom to oatmeal, meeting without seams.

Tailoring with the interfacing taken out, worn over true fluid layers.

One deep, submerged accent and one warm, rounded metal per look.

### Avoid

Hard black, optic white, and icy pastels, the three fastest edges back in.

Crisp, bonded, or knife-creased fabrics that argue with everything you own.

The sofa drift: softness with no line left anywhere.

### Consider

The melt of every piece. Ask whether it moves with you and resettles. A garment that keeps its own posture is not keeping you company.

Focusing on cost-per-wear. An unlined flannel blazer worn soft over five years costs less than three stiff ones abandoned in month two.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. Softness ages beautifully; limpness just ages. Ask which one you are holding before you buy it.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories carry your formula's final term, the touch of quiet depth that finishes your soft structure and blurred edges. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look, both of them warm and quiet.

Scarves and wraps are your first category. Brushed cashmere or washed silk in mushroom, fawn, or dark plum, worn loose so the edges of the outfit stay soft all the way to your jaw.

Bags should be softly structured. A suede or tumbled-leather shoulder bag that holds a shape the way your blazers do, approximately and kindly, in walnut or bark with brushed bronze fittings.

Substantial rings are your quiet depth made permanent. One dark garnet or smoky quartz in brushed gold, worn daily until it becomes part of your hand.

Use the ripple test before leaving the house. Look at yourself and slowly raise one arm. Everything should move a beat behind you, together. Any piece that stays rigid while the rest of you flows, swap it.

---

## 8. The Pattern

Your approach to pattern is dissolved and warm. A print should look like something seen through moving water at dusk.

For tailored pieces like blazers and trousers, keep pattern in the weave: soft melange flannels, tonal herringbone in walnut and taupe, and brushed checks whose lines have gone gentle.

For fluid pieces like slips, skirts, and scarves, choose warm watercolour washes, faded botanical shadows, and tonal marbling in your mushroom-to-plum range. Every motif should end before its edge does.

Pass over sharp geometrics, high-contrast stripes, and anything printed at full saturation. Hard pattern is hard edge, and you have already declined those.

Rely on the dusk test. Look at the print in low evening light. If it settles into a warm, moving texture, it is yours. If it still insists on its outline, leave it for a sharper chart.
