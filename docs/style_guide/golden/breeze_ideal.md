# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Breeze

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress by proportion the way a musician tunes by ear. You can feel when a sleeve is a shade too heavy for a skirt, or when a neckline needs one small counterweight, and you adjust without being able to fully explain the maths. That instinct for balance is the engine of your style.

Your wardrobe rests on a graceful equilibrium. The core formula for your style is balanced proportions + light movement + one refined touch. Picture a featherweight grey knit tucked softly into a fluid midi skirt in gentle navy, with a silk scarf at the neck and one fine gold ring. That is your baseline.

You build your wardrobe sociably and in conversation, noticing what works on others, borrowing ideas, testing combinations. Your collection evolves the way good talk does, lightly and continuously.

Rely on the half-mirror test as your ultimate compass. Cover one half of your reflection, then the other. Each half should hold its own: neither all the weight on top, nor all the interest below. If one half is doing everything, rebalance before you leave.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour world is cool, light, and courteous. You thrive in tones that get along with each other, soft colours in easy conversation rather than argument.

Build your foundation with airy neutrals in balanced roles. Gentle navy and feather grey anchor your tailoring and knitwear. Soft white acts as your primary base canvas. Light taupe and rose-beige bridge warm and cool, and powder blue keeps the palette moving like weather on a fine day.

Your accent rule is charming and singular. Introduce one refined tone per outfit, muted rose, airy lilac, or a soft sage. A muted rose scarf against feather grey is a compliment paid quietly, and one compliment per outfit is the polite maximum.

When a look feels too cool or too careful, rely on warm cream. This is your relief colour. One cream layer takes the formality down half a step and makes the whole outfit easier to talk to.

Pass over heavy blacks worn wall to wall, aggressive neons, and dense earthy browns. They bully a palette whose entire gift is lightness.

Your biggest trap is prettiness without anchor. All-pale, all-soft, all-floaty reads charming for a moment, then forgettable by lunch. When the outfit starts to drift, give it one anchor: a sharply tailored blazer, a structured flat, or the gentle navy piece worn where the eye lands first.

---

## 3. The Textures

You evaluate fabric by lightness and grace. A material should move a half-beat after you do, like a good dance partner, and never weigh down the conversation.

### The Good

Your best materials rank by lightness, drape, and refinement:

**Crepe de chine**: The heart of your wardrobe, for blouses and dresses that move fluidly without clinging.

**Cotton voile**: Essential for summer shirts and layers, crisp enough to shape, light enough to float.

**Featherweight merino**: Your knitwear staple, refined at a desk and easy on a train.

**Garment-washed poplin**: Brilliant for shirting with softness in the hand and a clean line at the collar.

**Silk-cotton blends**: Perfect mid-layers, taking the sheen down to a glow and the weight down to almost nothing.

**Kid suede**: For shoes, small bags, and detail moments, adding a fine matt richness at delicate scale.

**Lightweight wool crepe**: Your tailoring choice, holding proportion without ever hardening into armour.

### The Bad

Heavy leather, stiff canvas, and bulky knits overwhelm your frame of reference entirely. They plant themselves where your style wants to circulate. If a garment feels like furniture, it is furniture, and you should not wear furniture.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing sets crisp lightness against fluid lightness. A voile shirt over a crepe de chine skirt gives you two different kinds of air in one outfit, structure and movement in perfect proportion.

Use the wrist-flick test before you buy. Take the fabric by the corner and flick it gently. It should respond instantly and settle gracefully. A slow, heavy settle means the fabric will always be half a beat behind you.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of balanced proportions + light movement + one refined touch travels into every setting with its manners intact.

### At Work

Professional settings reward your sense of measure. A lightweight wool crepe blazer in feather grey, a washed poplin shirt, and fluid trousers in gentle navy. The refined touch is a silk scarf or one fine ring. Colleagues will describe you as put together and never be able to name a single loud reason why.

### Intimate Energy

Evenings let the movement lead. Crepe de chine in muted rose or powder blue, a fine knit slipping off one shoulder by design, and the refined touch closest to the skin, a delicate chain or drop earring. Your version of allure is fluency, not volume.

### Daily Movement

Weekends stay light and legible. A market morning does not require abandoning proportion. Voile shirt, soft straight jeans or a fluid skirt, clean minimal trainers or ballet flats, and the scarf doing the day's one refined flourish.

The formula of balanced proportions + light movement + one refined touch holds in every room. Only the tempo changes.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware in your wardrobe is fine, precise, and diplomatic. It seasons the outfit; it never dominates the plate.

### The Metals

Your zone is mixed and delicate. Fine yellow gold and fine silver are both yours, worn together freely when the scale stays small: a thin gold chain with a slim silver ring is a conversation, not a clash. Choose polished for jewellery and softly brushed for buckles and bag fittings.

Skip chunky statement hardware, oversized chains, and heavy industrial buckles. Anything that would survive a shipwreck is out of proportion with everything you own.

### The Stones

Look for stones with light manners. White topaz, pale sapphire, peridot, and small pearls give sparkle at conversational volume. Set them finely and wear them in ones and twos. Avoid enormous cocktail stones; you are not carrying a chandelier to lunch.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test, tuned for your scale. Your pieces are fine, but fine and flimsy are different words. A thin chain should still feel dense and fluid, like mercury. If it feels like tinsel, it will kink, snap, and disappoint by spring.

---

## 6. The Code

A light-handed set of rules to keep your style in proportion.

### Lean Into

Building every outfit on balanced proportions + light movement + one refined touch.

Cool, airy neutrals warmed by cream and lifted by one muted accent.

Fine-scale mixed metals, gold and silver in polite company.

One anchor piece whenever the outfit starts to float away.

### Avoid

Heavy, bulky pieces that park themselves in the middle of your lightness.

More than one accent or more than one shining piece. Charm is singular.

Prettiness with no anchor, the outfit that everyone likes and no one remembers.

### Consider

The balance of every outfit. Ask which half of the mirror is winning, and even the score before you leave.

Focusing on cost-per-wear. A perfect crepe de chine blouse worn weekly for years is a better companion than five almost-right ones.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. Ask whether the piece would still be in proportion with your life in five to ten years. Graceful things age gracefully; gimmicks do not.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories deliver the last term of your formula, the one refined touch that completes your balanced proportions and light movement. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look, chosen for finesse rather than force.

Silk scarves are your signature category. A crepe de chine square or slim silk band in muted rose, powder blue, or soft sage, tied at the neck, the wrist, or the bag handle. It is the most efficient refinement per gram in your entire wardrobe.

Fine jewellery carries your polish. Thin chains, small drop earrings, slim stacked rings in mixed gold and silver. Worn as one composed set, it counts as a single touch; the moment it becomes a collection, it counts as noise.

Bags should be small and structured. A neat top-handle or compact crossbody in kid suede or smooth leather, in taupe, navy, or soft white, with fine hardware. The bag completes your proportions; an oversized tote would repeal them.

Use the too-much test before leaving the house. Look once, then remove the piece your eye went to second. What remains will be exactly enough.

---

## 8. The Pattern

Your approach to pattern is light, social, and evenly spaced. Prints should feel like good small talk, easy, charming, and never overbearing.

For tailored pieces like blazers and trousers, choose subtle glen checks, fine even stripes, and quiet birdseye weaves in feather grey and gentle navy. The pattern should keep a polite rhythm rather than make a speech.

For fluid pieces like blouses, dresses, and scarves, soft watercolour florals in cool tones, spaced fine stripes, and small tossed motifs on pale grounds are all yours, provided the contrast stays gentle and the repeat stays even.

Pass over aggressive graphics, heavy dark prints, and jumbo logos. They interrupt, and interrupting is the one thing your style never does.

Rely on the glance test. Look at the garment for exactly one second, then look away. If what remains in your mind is a pleasant impression rather than a specific shouting motif, the pattern has your manners and belongs in your wardrobe.
