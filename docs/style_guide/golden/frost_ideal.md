# Your Cosmic Style Guide

### Frost

---

## 1. The Blueprint

You dress in straight lines. Where others see clothes, you see architecture: the angle of a lapel, the fall of a trouser, the negative space between a collar and a jaw. An outfit succeeds for you when every line agrees with every other line.

Your wardrobe obeys a cool, exact logic. The core formula for your style is clean geometry + cool composure + one polished signal. Picture a sharply cut column coat in glacier grey over an ink black fine-gauge polo neck, with a single polished silver cuff catching the light at your wrist. That is your baseline.

You build your wardrobe with detachment, and that distance is a strength. You are immune to the panic purchase because you evaluate clothes the way you evaluate ideas, on structure first and sentiment never.

Rely on the fall test as your ultimate compass. Hold any garment up by its shoulders and watch how it drops. If it falls in one clean, uninterrupted line, it belongs with you. If it collapses into vague folds, let it go, whatever the label promises.

---

## 2. The Palette

Your colour world is cool, lunar, and precise. You thrive in shades with low temperature and high clarity, colours that look decided rather than felt.

Build your foundation with cool tones in defined roles. Ink black and cool slate anchor your tailoring and outerwear. Glacier white acts as your primary base canvas. Silver grey and steel blue bridge your layers, while frosted lavender adds the one soft note your geometry permits.

Your accent rule is singular and electric. Introduce one polished signal per outfit, electric cobalt, sharp violet, or a gleam of pure silver. A cobalt bag against an all-grey column is a complete sentence. Do not add a second clause.

When a look turns severe, rely on washed lilac. This is your relief colour. Used as a knit or a shirt under cool tailoring, it lowers the guard of the outfit by exactly one degree, which is all you ever need.

Pass over earthy browns, mustard, rust, and anything sun-baked. Warm mud dulls your clarity and makes your precision look accidental.

Your biggest trap is total ice. An outfit of unbroken grey on grey can read as absence rather than composure. When you catch yourself vanishing into the frost, add the polished signal, a mirror-finish cuff, a patent boot, or one line of electric colour, and the whole structure switches back on.

---

## 3. The Textures

You evaluate fabric by the quality of its surface and the discipline of its drape. A material must hold a line or pour like liquid; anything in between is indecision.

### The Good

Your best materials rank by their precision and their fall:

**Cool-touch crepe**: The foundation of your fluid pieces, falling in a single clean line every time.

**Sharp suiting twill**: Essential for tailoring that holds its geometry from morning to midnight.

**Fine-gauge merino**: Your base layer of choice, close to the body without a single wrinkle of excess.

**Silk charmeuse**: Brilliant for evening, a liquid surface that reflects light in unbroken sheets.

**Satin-backed wool**: Perfect for jackets that stay matt outside and glide frictionlessly over other layers.

**Glazed leather**: Your signal material for shoes and bags, polished to a deliberate, architectural shine.

**Technical suiting stretch**: Ideal for trousers that keep a knife crease through a full day of movement.

### The Bad

Fuzzy mohair, chunky rustic knits, and distressed anything work against you. Fluff blurs your lines, and artificial ageing contradicts a style built on intention. If a fabric looks like it has a past, it has no future with you.

### The Sweet Spot

Your ideal pairing sets a matt architectural surface against one liquid gleam. A matt crepe column dress with a glazed leather boot creates the exact tension your geometry needs.

Use the edge test before you buy. Fold the fabric over your hand and look at the crease it makes. A clean, sharp fold means the garment will hold your lines. A soft, reluctant fold means it will not.

---

## 4. The Occasions

Your core formula of clean geometry + cool composure + one polished signal is portable to every environment without modification.

### At Work

Professional settings are your natural habitat. A sharply structured blazer in cool slate over a glacier white shell, trousers with an unbroken crease, and one polished silver signal at the wrist or lapel. Your composure reads as competence because, in your case, it is.

### Intimate Energy

Evenings let your surfaces speak. Silk charmeuse in ink or steel blue, cut on clean lines, with the polished signal moved closer to the skin, a sculptural earring or a slim mirror-finish collar. Cool never means cold; it means deliberate.

### Daily Movement

Weekends keep the geometry and drop the formality. A trip to the dry cleaner does not suspend your standards. Fine merino, straight dark trousers, minimal white leather trainers kept immaculate, and the signal riding along as sharp sunglasses or a clean silver ring.

The formula of clean geometry + cool composure + one polished signal is constant. Only the setting rotates around it.

---

## 5. The Hardware

Hardware is where your one polished signal most often lives. It is not an afterthought for you; it is the point.

### The Metals

Your zone is cool and gleaming. Polished silver, platinum, white gold, and mirror chrome are yours without apology. These finishes hold and return light with precision, exactly like the rest of your wardrobe. A high-shine chrome watch or a polished silver collar is your version of a raised voice: rare and effective.

Skip antiqued brass, hammered copper, and anything artificially aged. Manufactured history reads as clutter against a style built entirely of present tense.

### The Stones

Look for stones with clarity and exact cuts. White diamonds, clear sapphires, aquamarines, and grey spinel deliver light in straight lines. Choose bezel and channel settings that continue the geometry. Avoid cloudy cabochons and raw crystals; softness belongs in your knitwear, not your stones.

### Tip

Always apply the pick it up weight test. A polished piece that feels hollow is a prop, not a signal. Real quality has density you can feel before you see it. If the shine writes a cheque the weight cannot cash, put it down.

---

## 6. The Code

A precise reference to keep your style composed and decisive.

### Lean Into

Building every outfit on clean geometry + cool composure + one polished signal.

Cool tonal layering, glacier white through silver grey to ink black.

Mirror and high-polish finishes deployed as a single deliberate accent.

Garments that hold their line without your intervention.

### Avoid

Warm, earthy tones that muddy your clarity.

Fluffy, fuzzy, or distressed textures that blur your edges.

Piling on two or three competing metallics. One signal, then silence.

### Consider

The fall of every garment. Ask whether it drops in one line. If it hesitates, so should you.

Focusing on cost-per-wear. One perfectly cut cool slate coat, worn two hundred times, outperforms five approximate ones.

Checking for five to ten year longevity. Geometry does not date. Ask whether the piece will still be this exact in five to ten years, and only proceed on a yes.

---

## 7. The Accessory

Accessories carry your formula's final term, the one polished signal that completes your clean geometry and cool composure. The principle is strict: stick to one or two strong pieces per look, and let exactly one of them shine.

Sculptural jewellery is your headline category. A single polished silver cuff, a rigid collar necklace, or one architectural earring worn alone. The piece should look like a small building you decided to wear.

Eyewear gives your face its architecture. Choose sharp, thin metal frames in silver or gunmetal, or crisp geometric acetate in crystal grey. The line of the frame should agree with the line of your brow.

Bags must be structured and exact. A rigid top-handle or a clean rectangular crossbody in glazed black or slate leather, with polished hardware that operates like a precision instrument.

Use the one-signal test before leaving the house. Count the pieces that shine. If the answer is more than one, retire the weakest until only the strongest remains.

---

## 8. The Pattern

You treat pattern as drafting, not decoration. Lines, grids, and blocks are welcome; anything hand-drawn is not.

For tailored pieces like coats, blazers, and trousers, choose fine pinstripes, precise chevrons, and clean windowpane checks in cool tones. The lines must be straight, evenly spaced, and confident.

For fluid pieces like silk shirts and dresses, use large-scale colour blocking or abstract linear prints in glacier, steel, and cobalt. The geometry can grow in scale as the fabric softens.

Pass over rustic plaids, ditsy florals, and blurry watercolours. Softened, nostalgic pattern contradicts every decision you have made above.

Rely on the straight-edge test. Stand back and follow any line in the pattern with your eye. If it stays crisp and true at three metres, it passes. If it wavers into fuzz, it fails.
